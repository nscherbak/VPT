▶ New to this? What tones, semitones & sharp/flat mean

Notes

Music uses seven letter names — A B C D E F G — that repeat over and over as you go higher. A note written like C4 means "the C in the 4th octave"; the higher the number, the higher the pitch.

Semitone (half step)

The smallest normal step between two notes — the distance from one key to the very next key on a piano, black or white. For example C → C♯ is one semitone. Most letter-to-letter steps are two semitones, but E → F and B → C are only one.

Tone (whole step)

Two semitones stacked together. C → D is one tone; C → E is two tones. When this app says you sang "one and a half tones lower," it means your note was three semitones below the target.

Sharp & flat

These are the musical words for small pitch differences. Sharp (♯) means a little too high; flat (♭) means a little too low. As symbols they also name the in-between notes (the piano's black keys), like F♯. In singing, "you're a bit sharp" just means "you're singing slightly above the note."

Cents

A fine ruler for pitch: one semitone is divided into 100 cents, so a whole tone is 200 cents. A difference under about 15 cents is so small most ears hear it as "in tune."
